What is Prompt Engineering?
Why how you ask matters as much as what you ask
Unlocked ⌄

Definition

Prompt Engineering is the skill of designing inputs (prompts) to get the best possible outputs from AI language models. It's part art, part science — and one of the most valuable skills you can learn right now.

Why It Matters

Two people using the same AI can get completely different results just because of how they phrase their request. A well-crafted prompt means the difference between a generic response and something genuinely useful.

  • Bad prompt: "Write about marketing"
  • Good prompt: "Write a 200-word Instagram caption for a Nigerian fintech startup targeting young professionals aged 22–30, promoting a zero-fee money transfer feature. Tone: confident, friendly, relatable."

Who Needs This Skill?

Everyone using AI tools — developers, writers, marketers, students, entrepreneurs. As AI embeds in every job, knowing how to direct it effectively is a superpower.

Anatomy of a Great Prompt
The 5 elements every strong prompt needs
Locked ⌄

The 5 Core Elements

  • Role — Tell the AI who to be ("Act as a senior software engineer")
  • Task — What exactly you want ("Write a function that...")
  • Context — Relevant background ("This is for a mobile app with slow internet")
  • Format — How you want the output ("Return a numbered list")
  • Constraints — What to avoid ("Don't use technical jargon")

You Don't Always Need All 5

Simple tasks need simple prompts. Use all 5 elements for complex, high-stakes outputs like business documents, code, or creative writing.

Example Prompt

Role: You are an experienced Nigerian business consultant. Task: Write a cold email to a potential investor. Context: My startup helps farmers sell directly to restaurants in Lagos. Format: 3 short paragraphs, professional but warm. Constraints: No buzzwords. Max 150 words.

Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot Prompting
Using examples to dramatically improve outputs
Locked ⌄

Zero-Shot Prompting

Asking the AI to do something without giving any examples. Works well for simple tasks. Example: "Translate this to Yoruba: Good morning, how are you?"

Few-Shot Prompting

Giving the AI 2–5 examples of what you want before asking it to do the task. This dramatically improves quality and consistency.

  • Especially useful for classification or labelling tasks
  • Helps match a specific style or format you want
  • Use when zero-shot gives inconsistent results

When to Use Few-Shot

  • When you need a specific format consistently
  • When you have a unique style or voice to match
  • When doing classification or labelling tasks

Chain of Thought Prompting
Making AI reason step by step for better results
Locked ⌄

What is Chain of Thought?

Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting asks the AI to show its reasoning step by step before giving a final answer. This dramatically improves accuracy on complex problems — especially math, logic, and analysis.

The Magic Phrase

Simply adding "Let's think through this step by step." to your prompt can double accuracy on reasoning tasks. It forces the model to break the problem into steps before concluding.

When to Use CoT

  • Math and calculation problems
  • Logical reasoning and deduction
  • Business analysis and decision making
  • Debugging code

Why It Works

LLMs are better at arithmetic and logic when they write out each step — the same way a human performs better when working through a problem on paper rather than guessing.

System Prompts & Personas
Shaping AI identity and behaviour from the start
Locked ⌄

What is a System Prompt?

A system prompt is an instruction given to the AI at the start that defines its role, personality, and rules for the entire conversation. It's like giving the AI a job description before any work begins.

Creating Powerful Personas

  • Give the AI a name and role
  • Define its expertise and knowledge level
  • Set its tone (formal, friendly, technical)
  • Give it constraints (what NOT to do)
  • Define its goal (what it's trying to help with)

Why This Matters for Builders

When you build an AI app, the system prompt is your most powerful tool. A well-designed system prompt turns a generic AI into a specialist that feels custom-built for your users. Most AI startups differentiate through their system prompt — not the underlying AI model.

Prompt Patterns & Templates
Reusable frameworks for common use cases
Locked ⌄

The RTF Template (Role, Task, Format)

One of the most reliable prompt patterns. You specify who the AI should be, what it should do, and how it should structure the output.

The Rewrite Template

Start with your draft text, then instruct the AI to rewrite it with specific improvements: "more professional", "simpler", "shorter", or "more persuasive" — while keeping the core message.

The Explain Template

"Explain [concept] as if I am [a 10-year-old / a beginner / a business person]. Use a simple analogy and give one real-world example." This ensures you always get appropriately levelled explanations.

The RISEN Framework

  • Role, Instructions, Steps, End goal, Narrowing

Why Templates Matter

Templates remove guesswork. Instead of starting from scratch every time, you fill in the blanks of a proven structure — saving time and getting better results consistently.

07 ✓
Iterative Prompting
Refining outputs through conversation
Locked ⌄

Don't Expect Perfection on the First Try

Expert prompt engineers rarely get what they want in one shot. The real skill is guiding the AI through a conversation to progressively refine the output.

Iterative Refinement Techniques

  • Be specific about what's wrong: "This is too formal. Make it sound like a friend talking."
  • Ask for variations: "Give me 3 different versions of this."
  • Build incrementally: "Now add a section about pricing."
  • Constrain further: "Cut this to 100 words while keeping the key points."

The Feedback Loop

Write initial prompt → Review output → Identify what's wrong → Give specific feedback → Review improved output → Repeat until satisfied.

Pro Tip

Save your best prompts in a personal library. Build a collection you can reuse and remix for different tasks. This is how top prompt engineers work.

Prompting for Code
Using AI as your personal coding assistant
Locked ⌄

AI is a Powerful Coding Partner

With good prompts, AI can write, debug, explain, and refactor code. This is especially powerful for beginners who are still learning.

The Code Generation Prompt

Specify: language, what the function should do, input/output, any edge cases, and whether you want comments explaining each line.

The Debug Prompt

Paste your broken code and ask: "Find the bug and fix it. Explain what was wrong and why your fix works." Always include the error message you're seeing.

The Explain Prompt

"Explain this code line by line in simple English. Assume I am a beginner who knows basic HTML and CSS." — This helps you learn while getting help.

The Refactor Prompt

"Refactor this code to be cleaner and more readable. Keep the same functionality but use modern JavaScript (ES6+). Explain each change you made."

Common Prompt Mistakes
What not to do and how to fix it
Locked ⌄

Mistake 1: Being Too Vague

Bad: "Write something about my business." Good: "Write a 3-sentence elevator pitch for my mobile laundry delivery service targeting busy professionals in Abuja."

Mistake 2: No Format Specified

Bad: "Give me marketing ideas." Good: "Give me 5 marketing ideas as a numbered list. Each idea should have a one-sentence explanation and cost estimate."

Mistake 3: Asking Too Many Things at Once

Break complex requests into separate prompts. Don't ask for a business plan, marketing strategy, financial projection, and pitch deck all in one prompt.

Mistake 4: Not Providing Context

Context is the difference between a generic response and one that actually applies to your situation. Always include: who you are, what the product is, who the audience is.

Mistake 5: Accepting the First Output

Always review and iterate. The first response is rarely the best. Push back, ask for improvements, and refine.

Building Your Prompt Library
Creating a personal system for AI productivity
Locked ⌄

What is a Prompt Library?

A personal collection of tested, proven prompts organized by use case. Think of it as your AI toolkit — prompts you can reuse, remix, and share.

Categories to Build

  • Writing — Emails, social media, blog posts
  • Coding — Debugging, explaining, generating
  • Business — Plans, pitches, analysis
  • Learning — Explain concepts, quiz me, summarize
  • Creative — Stories, scripts, ideas

Template Structure

For each prompt in your library: Name, Use Case, the Prompt itself (with [PLACEHOLDERS] for variables), and an Example Output showing what good looks like.

Your Challenge

Write 5 prompts for tasks you do regularly. Test them, refine them, and save them. You now have the foundation of your personal prompt library — a professional tool that will grow with you.
